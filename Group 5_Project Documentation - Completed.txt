Introduction
	The Philippines is currently facing a profound educational challenge regarding reading proficiency among young learners. Recent global and regional assessments, such as the 2018 and 2022 Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) and the 2019 Southeast Asia Primary Learning Metrics (SEA-PLM), consistently place Filipino students among the lowest performers in reading literacy and comprehension (Abril et al., 2022; Balisoro et al., 2025; Librea et al., 2023). 
In response, the Department of Education (DepEd) mandates the Philippine Informal Reading Inventory (Phil-IRI) program to assess students' reading levels—categorized as Independent, Instructional, or Frustration—and identify areas for remediation (Abril et al., 2022; Balisoro et al., 2025). However, the manual administration of the Phil-IRI is highly inefficient. Teachers handling large classes must manually score the Group Screening Test (GST), compute reading speeds and comprehension percentages, and manually map specific reading miscues to appropriate intervention strategies. This creates a massive administrative bottleneck that frequently stalls the actual delivery of reading remediation.
Reading is the cornerstone of all academic learning. When a child struggles with reading, their overall intellectual, psychological, and academic development is severely compromised (Librea et al., 2023). Studies highlight that the effective implementation of the Phil-IRI relies heavily on the subsequent reading interventions; however, because the manual computation and diagnostic profiling are so time-consuming, teachers often treat the assessment merely as a paperwork compliance task for documentation rather than an actionable diagnostic tool (Abril et al., 2022). Automating this process is critical because it eliminates human error and drastically reduces the administrative workload. By shifting the teachers' focus from manual tallying to actual instruction, struggling students can immediately receive the precise, data-informed, and tailored pedagogical interventions they need to overcome their learning barriers.
The application domain for this research is Educational Technology (EdTech), specifically focusing on the deployment of Rule-Based Expert Systems for automated pedagogical assessment, academic profiling, and data-driven reading intervention planning within the Philippine public elementary school system.
Objectives
This project specifically aims:
To digitize the Phil-IRI Group Screening Test (GST) cut-off rubrics to automatically classify students as either exempted or requiring further reading assessment.
To develop a forward-chaining inference engine that accurately computes the appropriate starting reading passage level based on the student’s specific GST deficit.
To formulate a diagnostic recommendation module that analyzes inputted reading miscues, reading rate, and comprehension scores to accurately classify the student's reading profile (Independent, Instructional, or Frustration).
To prescribe targeted, DepEd-aligned pedagogical interventions (e.g., Marungko approach, phonics drills) tailored exactly to the root cause of the student's diagnosed reading difficulty.
To validate the system’s diagnostic logic, level-placement accuracy, and intervention relevance using institutional testing datasets evaluated by a licensed DepEd Public School Teacher.
Methods and Analysis
Problem Definition
Despite the DepEd mandate to utilize the Phil-IRI to diagnose and assist struggling readers, its current manual implementation is highly labor-intensive, error-prone, and inefficient. Public school teachers handling large class sizes of 40 to 50 students are required to manually administer the Group Screening Test (GST), determine adaptive starting passage levels, calculate reading speeds and comprehension scores, and meticulously identify specific reading miscues such as mispronunciation, omission, substitution, or word-by-word reading.
Because this manual computational and diagnostic process is extremely tedious, it creates an additional stockpile of paperwork for already overburdened teachers. Consequently, the critical transition from assessment to intervention is often broken. Teachers struggle to accurately translate a student's quantitative test scores and qualitative reading errors into the specific, DepEd-aligned pedagogical interventions required to help them.
Therefore, the core problem ReadAssist aims to solve is the manual, inefficient, and error-prone Phil-IRI profiling process that obstructs the timely delivery of specialized reading interventions. The proposed expert system aims to eliminate this administrative bottleneck by automatically evaluating GST scores, accurately classifying reading profiles, and utilizing a deterministic inference engine to instantly prescribe targeted, policy-aligned pedagogical actions for struggling primary students.
Knowledge Acquisition / Knowledge Engineering and Validation
The knowledge base of ReadAssist was developed from three main sources: authoritative references, datasets, and expert consultation. First, the study drew its core rules from the Phil-IRI Manual 2018, the Handbook on Reading Intervention, and related literature on reading assessment and intervention. These sources provided the basis for the GST cut-off rules, starting-passage assignment, oral reading computations, reading-profile thresholds, miscue categories, behavior indicators, and intervention guidance. In addition, the structure of the Phil-IRI graded passages was incorporated into the system so that passage selection would remain dependent on grade level, language, assessment type, and passage set.
Second, the study utilized a hybrid dataset composed of institutional data and a synthesized dataset. The institutional data consisted of anonymized historical Phil-IRI GST records and reading profiles sourced directly from Ms. Ma. Lourdes J. Guillermo, a licensed public school teacher at Eulogio Rodriguez, Jr. Elementary School. These records provided realistic classroom-based cases that helped ground the system’s rules in actual reading assessment practice. Meanwhile, the synthesized or new dataset consisted of simulated student comprehension scores and varied reading miscues created to rigorously test the system against extreme and boundary cases that may not always be sufficiently represented in historical records. Through this hybrid dataset, the proponents were able to evaluate whether the encoded rules performed consistently across both authentic and stress-test scenarios.
Third, the study incorporated expert review and knowledge validation through consultation with Ms. Ma. Lourdes J. Guillermo and Ms. Ana Cristi S. Pangilinan, Principal III from Southville Elementary School. During the review process, the proponents presented the system’s GST logic, starting-passage rules, oral reading computations, reading-level thresholds, miscue interpretations, and sample diagnostic outputs. The experts then examined whether these rules and outputs remained faithful to the procedures and language of the Phil-IRI Manual 2018. Their validation helped confirm that the knowledge encoded in the system was appropriate for actual school use, particularly in terms of correctness, clarity, and practical teacher interpretation.
Knowledge Representation
ReadAssist represents knowledge as a combination of production rules, decision tables, and structured student facts. Each student record stores GST results, assessment session details, oral-reading miscues, comprehension answers, behavior observations, and diagnostic outputs, and the system then applies deterministic rules to these facts. 
The major knowledge representations used are GST decision rules, reading profile rules, miscue rules, behavior rules, and passage rules. In the GST decision rules, a GST score of 14 and above is treated as exempted from further assessment, while a score of 13 and below is marked for intervention and further assessment; within that range, a score of 0 to 7 means the starting passage is 3 grade levels below the learner's current grade, and a score of 8 to 13 means the starting passage is 2 grade levels below the learner's current grade.
The reading profile rules classify Independent performance as a word-reading score of 97-100% and a comprehension score of 80-100%, Instructional performance as a word-reading score of 90-96% and a comprehension score of 59-79%, and Frustration performance as a word-reading score of 89% and below and a comprehension score of 58% and below. The miscue rules treat each miscue as equally weighted in the oral-reading score, and the most frequent miscue becomes the basis for root-cause analysis unless a comprehension-deficit pattern is detected. 
Meanwhile, the behavior rules append observed behaviors as supporting guidance without replacing the main miscue-based diagnosis, and the passage rules ensure that passage lookup depends on grade level, language, assessment type, and passage set. In keeping with the system design, Grade 3 assessments are Filipino-only, while Grades 4 to 6 may use both Filipino and English.
Inference Engine Design
ReadAssist uses a forward-chaining inference engine. The engine begins with recorded assessment facts and repeatedly applies rules until no new conclusion can be produced. This approach is appropriate because the system starts with known student inputs and derives the final diagnosis from them.
The reasoning sequence begins with reading the student and assessment facts, then deriving words per minute from passage length and elapsed reading time. After that, the engine counts the total miscues from all miscue categories, computes word-reading accuracy using the total miscues and passage word count, and computes comprehension percentage using the number of correct answers and total questions. 
Once these values are available, the system classifies the word-reading profile and comprehension profile, combines the two profiles into a final reading profile, detects the dominant miscue pattern or comprehension-deficit pattern, retrieves the matching root cause, intervention, and detailed guidance, appends observed behavior guidance if needed, and finally generates an explanation trace that can be shown to the user.
The main formulas used by the engine are Reading Rate = (Total Passage Words / Reading Time in Seconds) x 60, Word Reading Score = ((Total Passage Words - Total Miscues) / Total Passage Words) x 100, and Comprehension Score = (Correct Answers / Total Questions) x 100.
The system is intentionally deterministic. It does not learn from data in the machine-learning sense; instead, it follows the handbook rules exactly so that the results are consistent and explainable.
System Architecture / Expert System Model
	ReadAssist is organized around four core expert-system components that work together as one workflow. The Knowledge Base stores GST rules, reading-level thresholds, miscue interpretations, behavior notes, and intervention guidance, while the Inference Engine applies forward-chaining rules to the assessment facts and generates the diagnosis. The User Interface serves as the teacher-facing front end through the roster, assessment, and report screens, allowing the user to enter student data, record assessment results, and review outputs in a structured way. The Explanation Facility is embedded in the report module, where the system displays the final diagnosis, root cause, intervention, detailed guidance, and reasoning trace so that the teacher can see how the conclusion was reached.
Beyond these core components, the system also includes the Roster and Screening Module, which manages students, GST scores, separate Filipino and English records, sections, grades, and intervention status; the Assessment Module, which records pre-test or post-test data, reading time, comprehension answers, miscues, behaviors, session date, language, and passage set; the Growth Tracking Module, which compares pre-test and post-test results to show progress over time; the Batch Validation Module, which accepts CSV or Excel files and compares system output with expert-labeled reading levels for validation; and the Storage Layer, which persists student data locally so the roster remains available between sessions.
System Application (Prototype)
	The prototype includes class roster management with add, edit, delete, search, and filtering options, and it also supports separate GST recording for Filipino and English with automatic handling of Grade 3 Filipino-only restrictions. In addition, the system provides automatic GST status assignment to Exempted or For Intervention, a reading-assessment workflow with timer, passage selection, miscues counting, and behavior checklist, automatic diagnosis generation with explanation text and intervention guidance, report generation with PDF export and a growth report for pre-test and post-test comparisons, and batch validation against expert-labeled records.

The system workflow begins when the teacher adds a student to the roster and enters the GST results. From there, ReadAssist determines whether the learner is exempted or needs further assessment, and if further assessment is required, the teacher opens the assessment module. The system then selects the proper passage based on grade level, language, assessment type, and passage set, after which the teacher records reading time, comprehension answers, miscues, and observed behaviors. Once the data is complete, the inference engine computes the reading profile and determines the root cause, then the system generates a diagnosis, intervention plan, and explanation trace. Finally, the report can be reviewed on-screen, edited if needed, exported to PDF, or compared with post-test results later.
The system is also illustrated through several example use cases. For instance, a Grade 5 learner with a GST raw score of 6 is marked for intervention, and the system assigns a starting passage three grade levels below the current level before recording oral-reading miscues and comprehension results to determine the final profile. In another case, a Grade 4 learner with strong word recognition but weak comprehension is classified through the comprehension-deficit rule, and the system recommends vocabulary development, background knowledge activation, listening comprehension practice, and rereading of the relevant text segment. Likewise, a learner who repeatedly substitutes or omits words receives a miscue-specific intervention such as phonics review, pacing support, or guided rereading depending on the dominant error pattern.




Application Presentation
ReadAssist operationalizes the Phil-IRI assessment process by transforming its screening, assessment, and diagnostic procedures into a structured, sequential, and rule-based workflow. First, the teacher begins at the Class Roster screen, where the learner’s basic information is entered, including name, section, grade level, and Group Screening Test (GST) results. At this stage, the system already performs its first decision. 
For Grade 3, only the Filipino GST is recorded because that is the applicable screening language. In contrast, for Grades 4 to 6, the teacher may enter both Filipino and English GST results. For learners in Grades 4 to 6, the system stores Filipino and English GST results as separate records and uses the lower score as the primary basis for screening status and recommended starting passage level. When Filipino and English GST scores are equal, the system records the result as a tie while still preserving both language records separately in the Class Roster. 
Once these values are saved, ReadAssist automatically checks the score against the Phil-IRI screening rule. If the GST score is 14 or higher, the learner is marked Exempted, which means no oral reading passage is assigned, and the learner remains under regular classroom monitoring. However, if the GST score is 13 or below, the learner is marked For Intervention, and the system immediately recommends a starting passage level for the next assessment.
Next, the application determines where the learner should begin in the oral reading phase. This recommendation is not random; instead, it is rule-based. If the GST score falls within 8 to 13, the learner starts two grade levels below the current grade. If the GST score falls within 0 to 7, the learner starts three grade levels below. At the same time, ReadAssist applies a lower limit so that the starting passage is never set below Grade 3. 
For learners in Grades 4 to 6 who have both Filipino and English GST scores, the lower score determines the initial placement because it reflects the weaker reading performance. In the assessment module, this primary result also determines the default assessment language, although both languages may remain available when both GST scores fall below the intervention threshold. For example, if a Grade 3 learner gets a Filipino GST score of 16, the output is Status: Exempted, so no graded passage is assigned. Similarly, if a Grade 4 learner gets a Filipino GST score of 13, the output becomes Status: For Intervention with recommended starting passage level: Grade 3. In the same way, if a Grade 5 learner gets a Filipino GST score of 6, the learner is also marked For Intervention, and the recommended passage level remains Grade 3, because the computed level cannot go lower than the Grade 3 floor.
After screening, the process moves to the Reading Assessment screen, which is used only for learners who require further assessment. Here, the system displays the assigned passage and captures the full set of assessment facts needed for diagnosis. These inputs include the assessment type, such as pre-test or post-test, the passage set, the language, the session date, start time, end time, reading time, the learner’s comprehension answers, the count for each miscue type, and any observed behaviors, such as word-by-word reading, lack of expression, hardly audible voice, or disregard for punctuation. At the same time, the screen shows the passage level, word count, and number of comprehension questions, so the teacher can see exactly what the learner is being assessed on. In other words, this second screen completes the raw data collection stage before the expert system begins reasoning.
Once the teacher submits the assessment, ReadAssist proceeds to the Expert Diagnosis stage. This is where the system transforms raw reading data into meaningful outputs. Specifically, it computes the learner’s reading rate, word-reading accuracy, comprehension percentage, and then the final reading profile. For instance, a Grade 4 learner with a Filipino GST score of 13 may be assessed using a 67-word passage, with 0 miscues and 5 correct answers out of 6 comprehension questions. Based on these inputs, the system computes a word-reading score of 100% and a comprehension score of 83%. Because both scores fall within the Independent range, the output becomes Reading Profile: Independent, Primary Issue: No dominant reading problem identified, and Intervention Title: Instructional Next Step. In this case, the recommendation is not remedial. Instead, the system advises the teacher to move the learner to a more challenging passage to locate the learner’s instructional level.
On the other hand, the system can also detect learners whose difficulties are more specific. For example, a Grade 4 learner with an English GST score of 12 may be assessed on a 67-word passage with 4 miscues and 4 correct answers out of 6 questions, where Omission = 2 and Substitution = 2. From these values, ReadAssist computes a word-reading score of 94% and a comprehension score of 67%. Because both scores fall within the Instructional range, the final output becomes Reading Profile: Instructional. After that, the system checks which miscue occurred most often. Since Omission and Substitution are tied, the diagnosis becomes Primary Decision: Miscue Analysis and Primary Issue: Omission & Substitution (2 tallies each). The system then retrieves the corresponding root causes and interventions from the knowledge base. Omission is interpreted as reading too fast or failing to decode the word accurately, while Substitution is interpreted as guessing based on visual similarity or context. As a result, the recommendation is to slow down, use a line marker, pay closer attention to word details, and avoid guessing.
A more severe case can also be demonstrated. Suppose a Grade 5 learner with a Filipino GST score of 6 is assessed using a 101-word passage and records 11 miscues, 3 correct answers out of 7 questions, with Omission = 4, Substitution = 4, Repetition = 2, and Insertion = 1. In this situation, the system computes a word-reading score of 89% and a comprehension score of 43%. Since both results fall into the Frustration level, the learner is classified as Reading Profile: Frustration. Then, because the most frequent miscues are again Omission and Substitution, the expert system selects Miscue Analysis as the primary decision and outputs Primary Issue: Omission & Substitution (4 tallies each). Consequently, the intervention remains focused on slowing down, tracking the line carefully, and avoiding guessing, but the explanation becomes stronger because the learner is already performing at the Frustration level under the Phil-IRI thresholds.
In contrast, ReadAssist can also recognize when the main difficulty is not decoding but comprehension. For example, a Grade 4 learner may record zero miscues on a 67-word passage but answer only 3 out of 6 comprehension questions correctly while also showing disregard for punctuation. Here, the system computes a word-reading score of 100% but a comprehension score of 50%. Because the comprehension result is much lower, the final profile becomes Frustration, even though the learner can decode the words correctly. At this point, the system does not choose miscue analysis because there is no dominant miscue pattern. Instead, it triggers Primary Decision: Comprehension Deficit, sets the Primary Issue to Comprehension Deficit, and identifies the likely root cause as Poor Vocabulary and Schema Activation. Therefore, the intervention shifts toward building oral language and vocabulary first, followed by listening comprehension activities and guided rereading. The observed behavior, such as disregard for punctuation, is still shown in the report, but only as supporting guidance and not as the main diagnosis.
The reason ReadAssist can produce these outputs consistently is that it follows a forward-chaining rule sequence. In chronological order, the system starts with the facts entered by the teacher. First, it derives reading speed using the total number of passage words and the reading time. Second, it adds all recorded miscues to get the total miscue count. Third, it computes the word-reading score using the formula Word Reading Score = ((Total Passage Words - Total Miscues) / Total Passage Words) x 100. After that, it computes the comprehension score using Comprehension Score = (Correct Answers / Total Questions) x 100. It also derives the reading rate using Reading Rate = (Total Passage Words / Reading Time in Seconds) x 60.
After computing the numeric values, the system classifies the learner into Phil-IRI profile levels. For word reading, a score of 97% to 100% is Independent, 90% to 96% is Instructional, and 89% and below is Frustration. For comprehension, 80% to 100% is Independent, 59% to 79% is Instructional, and 58% and below is Frustration. Then, rather than averaging the two, ReadAssist takes the lower of the two profile levels as the learner’s final reading profile. This is important because a learner should not be considered strong overall if either word recognition or comprehension is already weak.
Finally, the system decides what kind of recommendation to generate. If the learner is Independent and shows no dominant miscues, the output becomes an Instructional Next Step, meaning the learner should move to a more difficult passage. If the learner’s word-reading profile is Independent or Instructional but the comprehension profile is Frustration, the system selects Comprehension Deficit and recommends vocabulary-building and schema activation support. However, if there is a clear highest-frequency miscue, the system chooses Miscue Analysis and retrieves the corresponding root cause, intervention, and detailed guidance from the knowledge base. When two or more miscues tie for the highest frequency, the system combines them into one primary issue, such as Omission & Substitution, and presents both interventions together. Lastly, any observed behaviors are appended as supporting guidance, so they enrich the explanation without replacing the main rule-based diagnosis.
Expert Validation
	The evaluation of ReadAssist was carried out through the professional review of Ms. Ma. Lourdes J. Guillermo, a Licensed DepEd Public School Teacher from Eulogio Rodriguez, Jr. Elementary School, and Ms. Ana Cristi S. Pangilinan, Principal III from Southville Elementary School. Their experience in elementary reading assessment made them well qualified to assess whether the system was aligned with Phil-IRI procedures and appropriate for school use.
During the review, the experts examined the correctness of the system’s key decision points, particularly the GST cut-off rules, starting-passage assignment, oral reading computations, reading profile thresholds, and miscue-to-intervention mapping. They also considered whether the explanation facility presented the basis of each result clearly enough for teachers to interpret and use in instructional planning.
The review was informed by a hybrid dataset composed of institutional data and a synthesized dataset. The institutional data included anonymized historical Phil-IRI GST records and reading profiles provided by Ms. Ma. Lourdes J. Guillermo, while the synthesized dataset contained simulated comprehension scores and varied reading miscues prepared to broaden the range of cases examined. Using these materials, the experts assessed whether the system’s outputs were accurate, logically consistent, and appropriate for instructional decision-making.
Based on their observations, several refinements were recommended. The experts advised that the terminology used in the interface and reports should remain consistent with standard Phil-IRI language. They also recommended that, for learners in Grades 4 to 6, the Filipino and English GST results should be retained as separate records in the Class Roster, even when the lower score is used as the basis for screening status and starting passage placement. In addition, they noted that the explanation facility should more clearly show why a learner was classified as Independent, Instructional, or Frustration, while intervention statements should remain practical, teacher-friendly, and directly applicable in classroom instruction. Finally, they affirmed that observed behaviors should serve only as supporting guidance and should not replace the main diagnosis derived from Phil-IRI scoring rules and the learner’s dominant reading difficulty.
